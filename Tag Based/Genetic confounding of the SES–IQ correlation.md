# Why SES correlates with IQ despite null shared-environment effects

Twin and adoption studies ([[Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart]], [[Texas Adoption Project]]) find the shared family environment contributes almost nothing to adult intelligence — adopted children's IQ tracks their **biological** parents, not their adoptive ones, so $c^2 \approx 0$. Yet parental SES still correlates reasonably with child IQ. These don't conflict, because they measure different things: $c^2$ is the *causal* effect of the rearing family with genes held constant; the SES correlation is a *observational* association with genes left in.

IQ strongly predicts educational and occupational attainment, so high-ability parents tend to reach high SES and pass IQ genes to their children. SES and child IQ therefore correlate largely because both are downstream of the parents' genotype, which the child inherits.

Adoptive (and twin-placement) families skew middle-class. Environmental effects on IQ concentrate at the low end (malnutrition, stress, lack of stimulation).

In addition, $c²$ is meaningfully positive in childhood and fades toward zero by adulthood ([[Wilson effect]]). SES–IQ correlations are often reported in children, where the environmental component is still live.
