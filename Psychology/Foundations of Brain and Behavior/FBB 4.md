Descartes: how does the brain communicate with senses? Electricity!

Epilepsy: seizures, burst of intense, rapid, abnormal electrical activity caused by abnormal discharges.

Symptoms: confusion, jerking, sudden falls, loss of consciousness, strange sensations and emotions.

Energy: positive -> negative
Neurons are negatively charged

History:
**Galvani:** applying electricity to frog leg
**Fritsch & Hitzig:** applying electricity to neocortex causes movement
**Bartholow:** same but in humans

EEG records electrical activity through skull

**von Helmholtz:** flow of information is too slow to be considered electricity, **ions** are the basis instead.

**Hodgkin & Huxley:** Squids (very big axons), oscilloscope, microelectrodes

**Na+** and **Cl-** outside of the neurons
**K+** and **A-** inside

3 ways: open, close, pump

**Resting potential:** electrical charge across the cell membrane in the absence of stimulation at **-70 mV**

Excess negative energy inside

**A-** always inside because membrane is impermeable to large molecules

**Depolarization**: becoming more positive
**Repolarization & Hyperpolarization**: becomes more negative

1. Small stimulus caused by neurotransmitters
2. Depolarization: Na+ goes in, cell becomes positive
3. K+ start going out (gate opens slower) causing depolarization (towards equilibrium(
4. Fast Na+ gates close
5. Hyperpolarization: cell becomes too negative due to extra K+ going out

Action potential: **reversal in polarity** of axon
Threshold potential: voltage at which AP is triggered, **-50 mV**

Na+ are more sensitive (open sooner), they close while K+ is opening

TTX (from pufferfish) **blocks Na+ gates**
TEA (synthetic drug) blocks potassium channels

**Absolute refractory period:** no stimulus given to neuron will lead to a second AP, happens from AP until after peak

**Relative**: strong current is required to produce another action potential

**Myelinated axons** are faster because little gaps (nodes of Ranvier) are packed with Na+ channels, **regenerating the electric signal**

**Multiple sclerosis**: damaged myelin

**Excitatory postsynaptic potential (EPSP):** brief depolarization of neuron in response to stimulus (more likely to produce AP)
**Inhibitory postsynaptic potential (IPSP):** hyperpolarization (less likely to produce AP)
These are caused by neurotransmitters.

Both of them are summed over time and space (close and at the same time)

If **summed** ionic **inputs exceed threshold**, AP will be initiated

**Backpropagation**: reverse movement created by AP (**plasticity**)

**End plate:** part of the muscle membrane contacted by axon terminal

**Acetylcholine:** neurotransmitter released at end plate by axon terminal

Attaches to terminals, allowing Na+ and K+ to depolarize the muscle to threshold.
