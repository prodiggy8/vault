How does the nervous system respond to stimulation and produce movement?

Sensory pathways are **afferent**
Motor pathways are **efferent**

**Somatosensory system:** fine touch, pressure, pain, and balance; motor system closely related.
- **Spinal cord:** motor reflexes
- **Brainstem:** timing, control
- **Cerebrum:** voluntary movement

1. ==**Prefrontal cortex**== plans movement (executive function)
2. ==**Premotor cortex** ==organizes movement sequences
3. ==**Motor cortex (M1)**== produces specific movements

We perform movements to quickly to rely on feedback about the current one to perform the next.

Hence, the brain produces motor ==**sequences**==.
**Pipelining:** As one is being executed, another is getting ready

Some motor neurons disconnect from muscles in amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). **Neuroprosthetics** uses **brain-computer interfaces** to help people in conditions like ALS.

**Brainstem: Species-Typical Movement**

Experiment: chicken only reacts at the presence of context

The brainstem organizes adaptive movements: posture, standing upright, walking.

Damage can lead to **locked-in syndrome.**

==**Spinal Cord: Executing Movement**==

Relays messages between body and brain.
**Contains motor programs:** someone with injury could still walk in a conveyor belt purely based on reflexes.

CNS structures involved in movement are both ==hierarchical and parallel.==

Spinal cord goes: cervical, thoracic, lumbar, and sacral.

**11.2**

Motor cortex has a body map (**homunculus**). Hands and tongue are overrepresented because of fine movement.

The motor cortex also has movement categories (action map). The motor cortex encodes a lexicon of movements, not specific muscle twitches.
- **Whole body** movements generate increase in blood flow in the **premotor cortex**
- More precise movements are elicited in the **motor cortex**


There is **plasticity** and learning in the motor cortex!
- Damaged area retains size and ability if rehabilitated

Excitation of neurons happens **before** movement, hence the motor cortex takes part in planning the movement.

**BASAL GANGLIA**

Connects cortex, thalamus, brainstem.
Located beneath the frontal lobe.

Formed by: caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, and nucleus.

One function of the basal ganglia is to **modulate the activity of the cortical motor system.**

The basal ganglia is involved in **association learning, motivation, emotion, and motor control.**

BG Input:
- All areas of the neocortex
- Substantia nigra (dopaminergic system)

BG Output:
- Motor cortex
- Substantia nigra

The basal ganglia controls **movement force**.
Damage to caudate putamen: dyskinesias, unwanted witching (hyperkinetic).
Damage to basal ganglia can also cause loss of control over motor ability (hypokinetic).

**Cerebellum**

At top of brainstem
- Has **flocculus**, a small but dense lobe involved in eye movements and balance
- **Homonculus** organization
- Makes 10% of the brain, has 4x as many neurons and more folds

Improves movement control through **timing**
**Error correction:** compares intended movement with actual movement and makes adjustments

1. Cortex sends movement to spinal cord
2. A copy is sent to cerebellum
3. Sensory receptors report to cerebellum
4. Cerebellum has information about both planned and executed movement


**SOMATOSENSORY SYSTEM**

- Receptors
Distributed throughout the body
Dendrites are the receptors
**Mechanical** reception, not electrical

Larger number of receptors -> more sensitive

Two types of skin:
1. Hairy
2. Glabrous (palm of hand and feet, lips, tongue)

Types of receptor:
- Hapsis (fine touch) 
	- **Both**
- Nociception (pain, temperature, itch)
	- **Slow adaptation**
- Proprioception (body awareness)
	- **Rapid adaptation**

**Rapid:** responds briefly to the beginning and end of stimulus (large, well-myelinated axons)
**Slow:** responds as long as the stimulus is there

**Somatosensory cortex location:**
	Front of parietal lobe
**S1**
- receives information from **thalamus** (just like the LGN)
- Broadmann's areas 3-1-2: constructs perceptions from somatosensory information.
**S2**
- Behind S1
- Brodmann's areas 5-7: refines perception, projects to frontal cortex
- **Incorporates vision and auditory information**

There are **FOUR** homunculus: skin (slow), skin (fast), muscles, and joints (pressure)

Damage to S1 impairs:
- sensory thresholds
- body awareness (proprioception)
- hapsis
- simple movements

The first direct evidence that the cortex controls movement was provided by: **Gustav Fritsch and Eduard Hitzig**

The person MOST credited with mapping the human motor cortex was: **Wilder Penfield**
