**Chemical vs Electrical Synapses**

In electrical, presynaptic and postsynaptic membrane are fused. **Faster.**

1. Neurotransmitter is synthesized
2. Packaged and stored in vesicles
3. Transported to presynaptic membrane and released in cleft (Ca+ caused by AP)
4. Binds to other neuron
5. It's degraded or diffusion so that it doesn't continuously interact with the neuron

Reuptake (or astrocyte reuptake) can happen too.
They may interact with auto receptors in the presynaptic cell too.

Synapses happen in different parts (axon to dendrites to cell body)

**Excitatory synapses:**
- Dendrites
- Round vesicles, dense material
- Wide cleft

**Inhibitory synapses:**
- Cell body
- Flat vesicles, less material
- Narrow cleft

**Neurotransmitters:**
- Synthesized or present **in the neuron**
- Must be **released and produce a response** when neuron is active
- **Same response** must be obtained if it is placed in the target **experimentally**
- Must have a **removal mechanism**

**Renshaw Loop**

Motor neuron -> Renshaw interneuron
Motor neuron -> muscle

Renshaw interneuron -> motor neuron (inhibitory synapse)

#### Neurotransmitters

##### Small molecule

- **ACh**

Choline and acetate comes from food
Choline and acetate are **synthesized into ACh** by two enzymes (CoA & ChAT)

Enzyme (AChE) can also **break ACh down into acetate and choline.**

ACh found in neuron and muscle junctions

- **Amines**

Tyrosine -> L-Dopa (good for Parkinson's) -> **Dopamine** -> **NE** -> **EP**

Parkinson's pathway: substantia nigra to striatum (part of basal ganglia). These regions are important in movement.

**Serotonin**
- Synthesized from compound found in chocolate, oats, milk, red meat, and others.
- Regulates mood, appetite, arousal, respiration, pain perception
- Depression can be treated by blocking reuptake (increasing time in synapse)

**Glutamate and GABA:** brain's workhorses

**Main excitatory and inhibitory** transmitters, respectively

**Glycine:** inhibitory neurotransmitter in the Renshaw Loop

GABA comes from glutamate

##### Peptides

**Act as hormones** that respond to stress
Contribute to **learning**

- **Oxytocin:** love, mother-infant bond
- **Endorphin:** pleasure, pain, regulates eating

Opioids mimic the actions of peptides (such as endorphin)

##### Lipid

Endocannabinoids: regulate other neurotransmitter's release

#### Receptors
**Ionotropic:** the ones where transmitters bind to open allowing influx and efflux of ions.

**Metabotropic:** binding site but **no pore**, linked to a G protein that act like a second messenger (to open an ion channel, for instance)

There are subtypes (some are specific to neurotransmitters), and neurons can have multiple

#### CNS Activating Systems

1. **Cholinergic:**
	- Maintaining attention
	- Role in memory
	- Death of neurons causes Alzheimer
	
2. **Dopaminergic**
	- Nigrostriatal pathways
	- Mesolimbic pathway: motivation, habits
	- Increase in dopamine causes schizophrenia
	- Decrease in dopamine causes ADHD
	
3. **Noradrenergic**
	- Emotional tone
	- Decrease causes depression, hyperactivity
	- Increase causes mania

4. **Serotonergic**
	- Maintains waking EEG pattern
	- Changes are related to OCD
	- Decreases are related to depression

**Hormones:** made in brain, regulate body function and activity
Long lasting and slow (as compared to transmitters)

Hypothalamus produces as a response to stressors

PNS (parasympathetic) stimulates, SNS inhibits

Two responses to stressors (SNS is the protagonist in both):
**Fast**: epinephrine surge, burst of activity & cell metabolism
**Slow**: cortisol release, turns off unnecessary bodily functions

GH, gastrointestinal system, reproductive system, and immune system are turned off.

Hippocampus shuts down stress response (as it has cortisol receptors)

**Chronic stress:** cortisol damages the hippocampus (slow) OR amygdala grows too much (fast)

PTSD caused by this
