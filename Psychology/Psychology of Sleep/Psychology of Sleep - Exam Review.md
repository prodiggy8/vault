**Central Nervous System (CNS):** brain and spinal cord
**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** All parts except brain and spinal cord

- **Peripheral Autonomic:** nerves that connect to ==internal organs==
	- **Sympathetic:** FIGHT or FLIGHT
	- **Parasympathetic:** REST and DIGEST
- **Peripheral Somatic:** nerves that interconnect brain and ==muscles and sensory systems==
	- Sensory input
	- Motor input

**Nodes of Ranvier:** gaps found at regular intervals in myelinated axons, they are **recharge stations** for electrical signals

**Other cells**
- Glial cells provide raw materials and structure
	- Oligodendrocytes (CNS) and Schwann cells (PNS) form **myelin**
	- **Astrocytes** are star-shaped; maintain **blood-brain barrier**, do neurotransmitter cleanup
	- **Microglial cells** remove debris from injured or dead cells

**Neurotransmitter criteria:**
1. Synthesized in pre-synaptic neuron
2. Released in action potentials
3. Recognized by postsynaptic receptors
4. Causes change in postsynaptic cell
5. Blocking release interferes with cell's ability to affect a postsynaptic cell

**Important neurotransmitters:**
1. Acetylcholine: movement
2. Norepinephrine: mood and arousal
3. Serotonin: sleep and waking
4. Orexin: newly discovered!
5. Histamine

Sulci (valleys) and Gyri (hills)

**Other parts of the brain:**
- Pons: relay motor and sensory information
- Medulla: transition from brain to spinal cord; **respiration and heart rate**
- Cerebellum: motor coordination and control
- Reticular formation: regulates sleep and wakefulness

**Limbic system:** structures important for ==emotion and learning==
- Amygdala: emotional regulation
- Hippocampus and **fornix**: memory
- Cingulate gyrus: attention
- Olfactory bulb: smell

Ventricular system: CSF waste removal
- Series of ==chambers filled with cerebrospinal fluid==
- Brain has a **glymphatic system** that drains waste in CSF-derived fluids during sleep

Electrophysiology
- **Electrodes** can pick up activity of groups of neurons, and **single-neuron activity** can be calculated
- Single-cell recordings directly measure activity of single cell
- **EEG**
	- Less invasive using scalp electrodes; millions to billions of neurons
- **Structural Brain Imaging**
	- Computerized Tomography (CT): X-ray absorption at positions around the head
	- Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): capture radio waves that differ by tissue density
	- fMRI (functional MRI): detects small changes in brain metabolism in active brain areas

Tectum: orients organism in the environment
Tegmentum: movement and arousal


### the endocrine system

the endocrine system is a network of glands that produce and secrete **hormones**, chemical messengers that travel through the bloodstream to regulate metabolism, growth, and reproduction. unlike the nervous system's rapid electrical signals, it coordinates long-term body functions by ensuring different organs communicate and maintain **homeostasis**.

### the pituitary gland

often called the **"master gland,"** the pituitary is a pea-sized structure at the base of the brain that monitors and controls other endocrine glands by releasing specific stimulating hormones. it acts as the critical link between the nervous system and the endocrine system, receiving signals from the **hypothalamus** to trigger or inhibit hormone production throughout the body.
