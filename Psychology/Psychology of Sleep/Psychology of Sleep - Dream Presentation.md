# Discussion


- Authors claim that they found partial support for the greater community-driven content in foragers -> only one community exhibited that
- More threat content was supported by both groups
	- More mortality and conflict associated content, other groups no difference
	- Hadza had less negative emotions -> more reprocessing

- Tribes have more danger, high child and adult mortality. Face illnesses, encounter with wild animals, parasites, conflict with other groups, etc.
- West is individualistic, faces ostracism, exclusion, loss of status, etc.
- Even with more danger, community drives better overcoming of threats, emotional regulation.

Patients with nightmare disorder should show more threats (even though in this study they did not). But cannot find solutions. Results in high fear and anxiety.

When the presence of threats in dreams is not associated with subsequent emotional resolution, as in recurrent nightmares, dreams seem to lose their emotional processing function.

Isolation and anxiety -> less emotional resolution (based on COVID data)

**Limitations**
- Future studies should collect daily activities, evidence of emotional regulation during daytime (is it dreams that's causing that?)
- Can't provide causality between dreams and wake
- Greater length for dream reports
- Bias in how participants remembered dreams (incentive)

**Hypotheses**
1. Dream content of both groups will have more community oriented content.
2. Will have more threats as well
3. Dreams will serve emotional regulation, lower anxiety and negative emotion
4. Nightmare group will have greater levels of negative emotions as well as the COVID group

**Prompt**

While the authors predicted that forager societies would show more social dream content, the results provided specific support: only the **BaYaka** exhibited a significant increase in community-oriented themes, likely due to their intense, daily reliance on cooperative kin networks for survival.

However, the support for **threat simulation** was universal across both forager groups. 

Both the BaYaka and Hadza showed a much higher frequency of mortality and conflict in their dreams compared to healthy Westeners AND people with SAD and nightmare disorder. 

The most impressive finding of this study is part of the discussion is the 'resolution' phase. Even though foragers dream of more threats, they experience **less** negative emotion. The researchers argue this is evidence of a healthy **emotional regulation function**.

They perform reprocessing and combining:

By linking a high-threat scenario—like being chased by an elephant—with a safe context or a successful escape, the brain 'extinguishes' the fear.

Another interesting finding is when this system fails.

By looking at the **Nightmare Disorder**: these participants actually faced just as much threat related content as the healthy control, but their negative emotions were significantly higher. The authors classify nightmares as **dysfunctional dreams**—they are simulations that lack resolution.

Similarly, the student sample during COVID-19 showed high anxiety and themes of **social isolation**.

The authors clarify limitations such as the inability to make causation claims, since this is a correlational study. We can't state that dreams caused emotional regulation.

The authors suggest future studies should focus on also observing the daytime routine of the foragers.



Ultimately, this study suggests that dreaming is an evolutionary mechanism designed to turn the threats into manageable memories and that this emotional regulation role is most effectively when we are socially integrated.
