Observational research: no manipulation of variables, just record whatever happens

E.g. Sleep deprivation => poor decision making, gets emotional, death after too much time

Cross sectional: relation between two variables measured at the same time (snapshot, example: FCEs)
	Easy, short, and cheap, but bias (e.g., recall)
	Can't get at causality (temporal precedence)
	More participants
	Different groups (sleep apnea vs insomnia vs no problems)
	
Types of cross sectional:
	Simple cross sectional: one group at one time
	Retrospective Case Control: 
	-> compare exposure histories of people with disease and without
	-> done when people already have the disease
	Good for studying rare diseases, when risk factors are objective and stable
	Same as above + selection bias (can't find a control with exactly same background)
	False positives! (many variables in the past)

Longitudinal: over time/different points in time (if FCEs were every week)
	People can get mad at the survey because of the repetition
	Same group over time

Types:
Cohort: follow people forward in time to see who develops insomnia (presents change in variable)
More accurate than case control because direct observation eliminates self-report biases
- Captures changes over time; temporal precedence; captures risk factors that can't be manipulated (e.g. exposure to radiation)
- Expensive, time consuming

Prospective case control: Recruits participants because they have or not exposure of interest and then follow them forward, e.g. ACEs.
Subset of it are the "natural experiments", when exposure happens randomly (car accident vs smoking)
- Exactly same as cohort
- Is exposure "random"? E.g. ACE not random, caused by confounders like SES, parent's ACEs

Experimental designs -> when a variable is manipulated!
Example:
- Andrew Elliot: color red affects students' performances in tests
- Hypothesis: Exposing to red on booklet would cause them to withdraw effort
- Assign red, gray (control), green booklets and measure!
	- Vocab and math tests, as well as ink color manipulation

Experiment: manipulation of one variable (IV) at least, at least one variable measured (DV)
Participants cannot assign themselves to the manipulated variable! Needs to be random!

Posttest-only design
-> assign, manipulate, and measure

Pretest-posttest design
-> measure, then assign, manipulate, and measure
-> controls for causation! did the manipulation cause the measurement?
Tests if groups were truly equivalent from the start!
(e.g. measure math at the beginning to ensure math ability did not influence result, only booklet color)
Can cause practice effect or fatigue effect!

Pretest also good if drop-out is concern -> so can control for drop outs, find attrition (the drop out factor)

Matched pairs design
To make sure groups are even, divide based on DV or variable that is known to be closely related to DV
E.g.: math ability and upbringing in CMU -> need to make sure same proportion of CS/BS and IS/BA students in each group
