Comprehension questions:

1. Using the words absorb, scatter, reflect, transmit, refract, photon, hue, wave, explain why we perceive a rose as red.

Light that comes from the sun **reflects** the surface of a rose and is **transmitted** to our eye. The light is then **refracted** through four transparent structures. Each part of the rose reflects light at a different **wavelength**. This difference is captured as **hue** by our cones. Our rods, on the other hand, **absorb** single **photons**.

2. Which are the four structures of the eye through which light is refracted?

The cornea, then the aqueous humor, then the lens, then the vitreous humor.

3. What is the function of the retina?

The retina is where light is actually transduced into electrical signals that our brain can understand to build our perception of vision. It contains photoreceptors that capture light and transmit the signals to retinal ganglion cells, that in turn send signals to the brain via the optic nerve.

4. Two reasons why is the fovea a special area of the retina?

The fovea is the very center of the retina. It has a very high density of cones (responsible for light perception, high acuity, and daylight vision) and no rods at all.

==**In the fovea, the other layers of the retina such as ganglion and bipolar cells are pushed to the side so light can hit photoreceptors directly, providing maximal acuity.**==

5. How many types of photoreceptors?

Two. The cones (responsible for daytime vision/bright lights, color differentiation, and high acuity) and rods (way more sensible, responsible for nighttime vision, only one photopigment so no color).

6. Are photoreceptors neurons, what is the main function of them?

Yes! Their main function is detect light and transduce light signal into electricity.

1. Graded vs action potentials?

Action potentials are discrete, they either fire or not given excitation that exceeds a threshold. Graded potential are continuous and vary in intensity.

2. When a photoreceptor ‘processes’/absorbs a photon, does it communicate with a bipolar cell
via a depolarized or a hyperpolarized graded potential? Is this excitatory or inhibitory?

It communicates via a hyperpolarized graded potential, meaning the synapse will have less glutamate. It's an inhibitory graded potential.

3. The textbook focuses on a concept called convergence. Take some time to understand what
convergence is and how it relates to how the rods and cones connect with bipolar cells.

Convergence in math means "everything leads to". That name resonates well with bipolar cells since "convergent" bipolar cells receive inputs from many photoreceptors. Divergent cells get this name because cones (the only photoreceptor connected to them) are individually connected to two divergent cells transmitting on and off signals to each respectively.
