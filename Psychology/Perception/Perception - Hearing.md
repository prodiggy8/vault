##### Sound

- **Amplitude** is perceived as *loudness* (measured in dB)
- **Frequency** is perceived as *pitch* (measured in Hz)

Humans ideally detect sounds between 20 and 20,000 Hz. dB is log scale, so ratio between faintest and loudest sound is over a million.

Sounds are created when objects vibrate; surrounding molecules causes pressure changes in medium. *Mechanic energy*.

##### Outer Ear

- Sounds are first collected by the *pinnae*, which is the visible part of the ear.
- Flow into the *ear canal*, funnel them to the tympanic membrane and protects the tympanic membrane.

**Tympanic Membrane** (a.k.a the *eardrum*)
- Thin sheet of skin at the end of the ear canal
- Vibrates in response to sound.

##### Middle Ear

- *Ossicles* amplify and transmit from tympanic membrane to oval window
- Malleus -> Incus -> Stapes
- **Lever** mechanism
- Muscles that **tense when sounds are loud**

##### Inner Ear

Where sound is transduced into neural signals.

- **Cochlea:** spiral structure containing the *organ of Corti*, filled with watery fluids.
- *Vestibular, middle, and tympanic* canals.

- Vestibular and middle are separated by the Reissner's membrane
- Middle and tympanic are separated by the Basilar membrane

**Path**

Ossicles -> push oval window -> moves liquid in vestibular canal
Remaining pressure -> helicotrema (u-turn in vestibular canal) -> leaves via round window

**Organ of Corti**

Contains hair cells and dendrites. 
Also includes *basilar membrane* and the *tectorial membrane*.

**Hair cells:** one row of inner, three rows of outer.
- *Stereocilia* are organelles that transduce sound.
- *Place code* is the tuning of different parts of the cochlea to different frequencies.
	- Membrane is stiffer in some places

- Inner hair cells transmit information
	- Much less!
	- Single row
	- Almost 95% of auditory fibers are connected to inner hair cells.
	- Afferent
- Outer hair cells receive information from the brain, *feedback system*
	- Can make parts of the cochlear partition stiffer

**Tectorial membrane:** floats above inner hair cells and touches outer hair cells.
- Vibration causes displacement, which bends stereocilia.


*Temporal code:* only fires at specific point (sound wave is at its top)
- Volley principle: each fire at a wave. Neurons can get tired with fast frequencies so it needs more than one.

Amplitude - cell recruitment, firing rate
Frequency - Place code, temporal code

**Cerebrum**

-> cochlear nucleus -> superior olive (inputs from both ears converge) -> inferior colliculus (suppress sounds of chewing, etc, modulates vision) -> MGN (relays to temporal cortex) -> A1

**A1**

Organized anatomically based on frequencies

Cochlear implants: coils, implanted in auditory nerve.

Threshold tuning curve: minimum intensity to elicit neural response
Lowest point is characteristic frequency: sweet spot of the fiber

Outer hair cells: cochlear amplifier
