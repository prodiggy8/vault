## Chapter 3

> [!quote] Ridiculous opening
> Indeed, in another life, I might have become a defender of the classical view of intelligence, like so many of my aging white male contemporaries.


Gardner goes on: "I found it comfortable to deem the capacities of those in the arts as fully cognitive-no less cognitive than the skills of mathematicians and scientists, as viewed by my fellow developmental psychologists."

A statement not very well supported by evidence: "People have wide range of capacities. A person's strength in one area of performance simply does not predict any comparable strengths in other areas."

He then picks the example of lesions in left hemisphere in right-handers causing language impairments ([[Wernicke's Area]]) versus damage in right hemisphere causing spacial orientation problems and proposes that it implies brain functions are separate with loose and unpredictable relations with one another. There are a number of counterexamples to this statement.
