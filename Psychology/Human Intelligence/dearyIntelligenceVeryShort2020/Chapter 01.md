## How many types of intelligence are there?

Massive study done by The Psychological Corporation (now Pearson) in collaboration with [[David Wechsler]]. Tested representative sample of the US on the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale.

- *Caveat:* W.E.I.R.D sample.

![[Chapter 01.png]]

Some depend on education; some are timed; some involved language, others numbers, shapes, or abstractions; some involved memory. They tap a wide range of mental functions. **Only 3 involve any sort of paper and pencil.**

<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Every single one of the 13 tests has a positive correlation with every other one.</mark>

Lowest correlation is $r=.3$; highest is $r=.8$.

There are sub-groups (*big circles in picture*). Vocabulary, similarities, information, and comprehension are especially strongly correlated between themselves.

The sub-groups are also correlated between themselves ($r \in [.6, .8]$).

>[!info] In short:
>G, sub-group constructs, and test-specific skills all play a role in determining performance on this tests, with g accounting for the majority of variation.

First person to describe *g* was [[Charles Spearman]] in a famous paper from 1904. He examined children's scores on school subjects and found they were all correlated.

Notably, [[Louis Thurstone]] suggested there were in fact 7 separate human abilities. However, **it became clear by the 1940s that there is in fact a general factor** that shows up every time we measure people on a collection of mental tests.

[[John Carroll]] analyzed all noteworthy studies on human mental abilities using the same statistical methods in his 1993 book [[Human Cognitive Abilities: A Survey of Factor Analytic Studies]]. The following is a synthesis of his findings, the *three stratum model*.
