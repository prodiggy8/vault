# Chapter 1
## How many types of intelligence are there?

Massive study done by The Psychological Corporation (now Pearson) in collaboration with [[David Wechsler]]. Tested representative sample of the US on the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale.

- *Caveat:* W.E.I.R.D sample.

![[Chapter 01.png]]

Some depend on education; some are timed; some involved language, others numbers, shapes, or abstractions; some involved memory. They tap a wide range of mental functions. **Only 3 involve any sort of paper and pencil.**

<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Every single one of the 13 tests has a positive correlation with every other one.</mark>

Lowest correlation is $r=.3$; highest is $r=.8$.

There are sub-groups (*big circles in picture*). Vocabulary, similarities, information, and comprehension are especially strongly correlated between themselves.

The sub-groups are also correlated between themselves ($r \in [.6, .8]$).

>[!info] In short:
>G, sub-group constructs, and test-specific skills all play a role in determining performance on this tests, with g accounting for the majority of variation.

First person to describe *g* was [[Charles Spearman]] in a famous paper from 1904. He examined children's scores on school subjects and found they were all correlated.

Notably, [[Louis Thurstone]] suggested there were in fact 7 separate human abilities. However, **it became clear by the 1940s that there is in fact a general factor** that shows up every time we measure people on a collection of mental tests.

[[John Carroll]] analyzed all noteworthy studies on human mental abilities using the same statistical methods in his 1993 book [[Human Cognitive Abilities: A Survey of Factor Analytic Studies]]. The following is a synthesis of his findings, the *three stratum model*. More sub-groups appeared because Carroll worked with a wider range of tests.

### Dissidents

[[Howard Gardner]]'s *[[gardnerIntelligenceReframedMultiple1999|Multiple Intelligences]]* theory suggests there are many unrelated forms of mental ability.

The facts are the some of his factors are **well known to be correlated with g**. Some other of his so-called intelligences, though valued human attributes, **are not considered** to be [[Mental Ability]], for instance physical intelligence is a set of motor skills and interpersonal intelligence involves personality traits.

A mental ability, as defined by [[Lee Cronbach]] and accepted as consensus by differential psychology and psychometrics is A _mental ability_, in Deary's sense, is a cognitive capacity measured by maximal-performance tests — tasks that have right and wrong answers, where the score reflects how well you can do _at your best_. It's a measure of capacity: how efficiently you process information, reason, manipulate symbols, solve problems. The defining empirical signature is that these abilities don't come out independent when you actually measure them — they form a _positive manifold_, meaning everyone's scores across different cognitive tests correlate positively, which is what gives rise to g (the general factor). So "mental ability" isn't just "anything the mind does"; it's specifically the family of maximal-performance cognitive capacities that load on g.
